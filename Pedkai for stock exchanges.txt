The Core Problem

Modern stock exchanges operate some of the most complex and latency-sensitive infrastructure in existence:
	•	Matching engines
	•	Market data distribution systems
	•	Colocation environments
	•	High-performance network fabrics

These systems are engineered for determinism, resilience, and microsecond-level performance.

However, over time, a critical gap emerges:

This is not due to failure — it is a natural consequence of:
	•	Continuous change and patching

	•	Legacy systems coexisting with modern stacks

The result is a hidden, evolving system state:

The Infrastructure Reality Gap

1. PTP / Clock Synchronization Visibility

What it is

Most exchanges rely on Precision Time Protocol (PTP) to keep all infrastructure components tightly synchronized (often sub-microsecond accuracy).

This includes:
	•	Matching engines
	•	Market data systems
	•	Network switches
	•	Capture/monitoring systems

⸻

The Problem

Even if PTP is “configured,” in reality:
	•	Some nodes drift
	•	Some switches degrade timing accuracy
	•	Boundary clocks behave inconsistently under load
	•	Failover paths introduce asymmetry

And critically:

You often don’t have a unified view of actual time alignment across the infrastructure.

⸻

What pedk.ai would do (within your constraints)

Without touching any order data, you can:
	•	Ingest PTP telemetry (offsets, delay, sync state)
	•	Correlate across infrastructure nodes
	•	Detect:
	•	Nodes drifting outside tolerance
	•	Inconsistent time domains
	•	Degraded clock hierarchies
	•	Asymmetric paths affecting sync accuracy

⸻

Why an exchange cares

Not for trade audit (you’re avoiding that), but for:
	•	Infrastructure consistency
	•	Deterministic sequencing of internal events
	•	Confidence that systems share a common time base

⸻

Positioning (important)

You are NOT saying:

“We validate regulatory timestamps”

You ARE saying:

“We ensure your infrastructure shares a consistent and stable time foundation”

⸻

2. Per-Hop Latency Observability (Network-Level Only)

What it is

Instead of measuring end-to-end latency, you break it down into:
	•	NIC → Top-of-rack switch
	•	Switch → aggregation layer
	•	Aggregation → core
	•	Core → destination system

⸻

The Problem

Most environments only see:
	•	Total latency
	•	Or coarse metrics (interface stats)

They cannot easily answer:
	•	Where exactly latency is introduced
	•	Which hop is unstable
	•	Whether routing changes affect performance

⸻

What pedk.ai would do

Using:
	•	Network telemetry (sFlow, NetFlow, INT, SNMP, switch counters)
	•	Timestamped infrastructure logs
	•	Path inference

You can:
	•	Reconstruct logical paths between systems
	•	Estimate latency contribution per hop
	•	Detect:
	•	Microbursts
	•	Buffer congestion
	•	Intermittent slow paths
	•	Route inconsistencies

⸻

Why this matters (without touching trades)

You’re not analyzing orders.

You’re answering:

“Is the infrastructure path behaving consistently and predictably?”

This helps:
	•	Network teams isolate bottlenecks
	•	Infra teams validate topology assumptions
	•	Ops teams understand degradation before failure

⸻

Key constraint alignment

You are observing:
	•	Packets and infrastructure behavior
NOT:
	•	Order contents
	•	Trade flow
	•	Participant activity

3. Hardware-Level Telemetry Integration (NICs, Switches)

What it is

Modern low-latency environments rely heavily on:
	•	High-performance NICs (Solarflare, Mellanox, etc.)
	•	Layer 1 / Layer 2 switches
	•	FPGA-assisted devices (sometimes)

These components expose deep telemetry, but it is:
	•	Fragmented
	•	Vendor-specific
	•	Rarely correlated centrally

⸻

The Problem

Critical issues originate at hardware level:
	•	NIC buffer pressure
	•	Packet drops before OS visibility
	•	PCIe contention
	•	Switch queue congestion
	•	Port-level microbursts

But:

These rarely show up cleanly in traditional monitoring.

⸻

What pedk.ai would do

Ingest and correlate:
	•	NIC stats (queue depth, drops, interrupts)
	•	Switch telemetry (buffer usage, ECN marks, queue occupancy)
	•	Port-level counters
	•	Hardware timestamps (if available)

Then:
	•	Align them with system-level behavior
	•	Detect patterns like:
	•	“Latency increase always correlates with NIC queue spikes on node X”
	•	“Intermittent drops tied to specific switch port under burst conditions”

⸻

Why exchanges care

Because many “mysterious” issues are actually:

Hardware-level phenomena that never surface clearly at system level

This improves:
	•	Root cause accuracy
	•	Troubleshooting speed
	•	Confidence in infrastructure behavior
